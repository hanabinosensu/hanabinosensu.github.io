About

Tokyo Hanabi is a yosakoi dance team formed in 2005 by a few Waseda students, and now boasts 150+ members. Yosakoi is a freeform dance and only two rules apply: hold two pieces of wooden clapper called naruko in hands, and feature the yosakoibushi melody in a dance song. Hanabi uses some extra props matoi and sensu in addition to naruko (see image below).

Amatsukasa

The 19th annual dance title “Amatsukasa” depicts a powerful dragon thriving in the sky 🐉 First revealed at Shonan Yosakoi Festival, it was awarded a grand prize.

Prologue: Amatsukasa is descending upon us ― the radiant dragon that once reigned and blessed the heavens.

Their sleeves sway like a dragon tail. Tokyo Hanabi is here, shaking the heavens in glory.

The sun sinks below the horizon. Mortals are fragile and ephemeral like bubbles on water.

The ancient tale says there once was a dragon that reigned and blessed the heavens. It rises into the moonlit sky and cloaks itself in the clouds at dawn.

One falls into slumber under the dim sky, and barely notices a silhouette against the hazy moon.

Lightning flashes across the faint horizon where heaven and earth merge. Amatsukasa, come emerge and ascend high here and now. Its thundering heartbeat echoes far and near. Let it traverse the sky and lead the way to daybreak. Wrapped in rushing winds, it pierces the clouds and soars into the very heavens, to embark on an eternal journey.

Anthem: Freshmen arrive and juniors depart ― quite transient like festival joy. Our life in Hanabi is fleeting, yet the time and memories we shared will last forever.

Bathed in bright light, it carries the hope through the starlit sky, resembling a single spark of a shooting star. Fly high and higher, Amatsukasa! It endures ever-circling seasons and aeons to celebrate the dawn after a good long night. The morning sun shines on eyes and falls onto earth. With grace and hope of people, Amatsukasa is setting out on eternal wanderings.

Epilogue: But what is Amatsukasa in the end? ― It is none other than Tokyo Hanabi XIX.
